The Yale Brand Documentary
The story behind Yale's Graphic and Brand Identity

ABOUT THE DOCUMENTARY

The Yale Brand Documentary is a story about how Yale University created its graphic branding identity for its Yale Corporation. Yale hired Pentragram's Michael Bierut, along with typeface designer Matthew Carter (designer of Hevetica Compressed, Georgia, Verdana), and a team of graphic designers to rethink how Yale's brand should look like to the global education community. Inspired by the Helvetica Documentary, I created a 40-min long documentary called "The Yale Brand" that featured designers such as Michael Bierut, Matthew Carter, Chris Pullman (former VP @ WGBH), and many more involved in the rebranding.

The story behind Yale's rebrand

THE STORY - TRIPS TO CHINA & THE MIDDLE EAST

"I had many trips to the Middle East, where people dealt with didn't know English. They could not read the word Yale. But they could understand what Coke was. They knew what IBM was. Why was that? They didn't know the letters either. But they knew the consistency of the word mark for these well known institutions." -- Linda Lorimer, Yale VP of Global and Strategic Initiatives

The story started when Yale President Rick Levin went on a trip to China and realized a problem: "if you didn't have the English alphabet that you're familiar with, how would you know Yale as Yale?" This was reinforced when Linda Lorimer, the Yale Vice President of Global and Strategic Initiatives, realized that when she travelled to the Middle East, many people didn't know English but they knew what Coke and IBM were, not because they knew the letters, but they knew the consistency for the word mark for these companies. This made them reflect on how Yale should redesign its graphic identity to support its brand as a global educational institution that is recognized by people from every country. They then hired Pentagram's Michael Bierut to redesign its branding, Matthew Carter to redesign its typeface, John Gambell and more to rethink its graphic identify.

They made a decision that instead of using a university emblem as the core logo, they would make Yale as "Yale" - the four letter words, designed in the Yale Typeface, would make the core of its brand.

Competing in the age of iTunes

Another reason for rebranding was the digitization of educational content and the rise of education media such as iTunes, where many people were downloading lectures and course materials. At that venue, users are looking at a selection of icons that are quite small, every brand had the same amount of real estate that to work with physically, and with such limited space, low resolution, and intense competition, the question is how we can resolve it in a way that makes it stand out. The four letter wordmark played an important role in branding in this field.

"When we have very little space, low resolution, very intense competition, because those icons were surrounded by other icons, how do we resolve that in a way that makes it stand out?" -- Michael Bierut, Partner at Pentagram

Further explorations - the Yale typeface and the Yale Blue

The documentary then explored the core of Yale's graphic rebranding — the Yale Typeface created by Matthew Carter, the typographer who also created Helvetica Compressed, Georgia, and Verdana. We explored his inspirations of the typeface from Bembo and De Aetna, which represented one of the breakthroughs in printing technology at that time in 15th century and is known for its synthesis of the capitals and the lowercase. The typeface had to be used in a variety of use cases with campus signs, marketing collatoral, and the web. We also explored the origin of the Yale blue and the Yale bulldog mascot.

Branding & Commercialization

The documentary then moved on to explore the dynamics between branding diversity and commercialization.

"Graphic design as a body of study in the United States and then afterwards a profession called "Graphic Design" was really invented at Yale, the School of Art, by Alvin Eisenman. His students included the people that would go on to design logos for Mobil Oil, Chase Manhattan Bank, Xerox, you know, Corporate America as it existed in the mid twentieth century. So the idea of branding, to a certain degree, you can trace to Yale." -- Michael Bierut, Pentagram
